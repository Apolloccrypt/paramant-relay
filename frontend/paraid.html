ParaID  Beta

Prove a claim. Keep the data.

A verifier asks a question, like “is this person 18 or older?”. Your device answers yes or no, with cryptographic proof. The passport fields behind the answer never leave your device, and the relay never sees them either.

  1. 1. An issuer seals the facts

    A trusted issuer writes each field into a salted, sealed hash and signs the set once with ML-DSA-65 (FIPS 204). The issuer identity is bound to that key as a DID.

  2. 2. Your device answers

    The wallet opens the sealed answer plus the key binding, and signs the verifier’s fresh nonce with the holder key. You see precisely what will be shared before it leaves the device.

  3. 3. The verifier checks offline

    The verifier recomputes the chain, checks the issuer signature, confirms the credential is bound to the presenting device and that the answer is fresh. It learns the answer and who vouched for it. Nothing else.

How to read the results

    

      
✓ 18+? YES

      
The answer itself, cryptographically proven, without showing the birthdate behind it.

      
issuer: did:paramant:…

      
Who vouched for the facts. The code is derived from the issuer’s signing key, so it cannot point to anyone else.

      
issuer signature: ML-DSA-65 valid

      
The sealed set of facts really was signed by that issuer, and nothing in it was changed afterwards.

      
holder: key bound ✓ · fresh nonce ✓

      
The credential is locked to the key of the device showing it, and the answer was produced just now for this specific check. A stolen or replayed proof fails here.

      
fields received: age_over_18 + key binding

      
The complete list of what actually reached the verifier. Everything else stayed sealed on the device.

      
issuer registered? yes: <name>

      
The issuer appears in Paramant’s public issuer registry, and every registration is anchored in the public transparency log, so it cannot be quietly added or removed.

      
issuer registered? no (demo)

      
This demo invents its own issuer inside your browser tab, so it is deliberately NOT in the public registry. Real credentials come from registered issuers only. The count shown is how many real issuers are registered today.

      
issuer registered? REVOKED

      
The issuer was withdrawn from the registry. The math of the proof may still be valid, but you should no longer trust credentials from this issuer. Revocations stay visible forever.

      
credential revoked by the issuer

      
The issuer is fine, but this specific credential was withdrawn (lost phone, expired document, error at issuance). Issuers publish a status list of revoked credential ids (opaque hashes, no personal data), and the verifier checks the proof’s root against it.

      
why the registry check makes 1 request

      
“Is this issuer registered right now?” is a question about a live, shared list (issuers get added and revoked), so your browser has to fetch that public list once. The request carries nothing about you, your document or your answer. What it buys you: the guarantee that the vouching party is a vetted, registered issuer rather than anyone who generated a key.

      
✗ rejected

      
One of the checks failed: tampered data, a key that does not match, an expired or foreign nonce. The verifier tells you which one.

      
network requests during demo

      
Stays 0 while you issue and prove. The optional registry check makes exactly 1 request, and the counter turning red on that click is the counter proving it works.

    

  


  

    
Beta means beta. The honest list:

    
    
      
  • The demo issuer is generated in your tab; it is not a real authority. Production needs identity-proofing at a trusted issuer.
    • 
      
  • No revocation yet: a real deployment adds a status list so issuers can withdraw credentials.
    • 
      
  • Repeated proofs from the same credential are linkable to each other. The zero-knowledge route (STARK over the same sealed structure) removes that; it is on the roadmap, not in this beta.
    • 
      
  • This is an advanced cryptographic attestation, not an eIDAS-qualified identity scheme.
    • 
      
  • This page makes no network calls. Open devtools: the network tab stays empty while you run the demo.
